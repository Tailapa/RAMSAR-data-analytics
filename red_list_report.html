Wetland Risk Assessment: The Red List

We define the "Red List" of Indian Wetlands as those high-value sites that achieve a Diversity Score of 5 or higher while simultaneously facing 20 or more unique threats . These sites represent the most ecologically significant yet vulnerable areas in the country. Based on the data, the following sites are under extreme pressure:

39 THREATS

Pallikaranai Marsh

This is the most stressed wetland in the current assessment. It is experiencing a total system assault, facing everything from urban sprawl and industrial pollution to sensory disturbances (light and noise) and severe climate events.

35 THREATS

Gulf of Mannar

A complex marine ecosystem where heavy industrial activity, international shipping lanes, and climate change pressures converge.

34 THREATS

Nandur Madhameshwar

These sites are suffering from severe fragmentation due to a high density of infrastructure, such as roads and utility lines, combined with relentless agricultural encroachment.

29 THREATS

Asan & Beas Reserves

These riverine systems are primarily stressed by hydrological disruptions—including water regulation, dams, and canalization—compounded by heavy recreational and tourism use.

The analysis reveals that India's Ramsar sites are not merely facing isolated environmental problems, but ompound crises. Sites like the Pallikaranai Marsh are navigating nearly 40 distinct threats simultaneously. As a wetland falls within an urban or industrial sphere, the number of threats does not simply increase—it multiplies. This suggests that traditional conservation methods, which focus solely on protecting the water body itself, are insufficient. To preserve these high-value areas, management must shift toward integrated mitigation strategies that address the surrounding urban and industrial infrastructure as part of the broader ecosystem.

Actionable Policy Recommendations

01 Make the Ramsar Red List a legal priority class
  • Notify Red List sites as “priority wetlands” under State Rules and management plans.

    Use the threshold of diversity score ≥5 and threats ≥20 to create a formal Priority Category, with mandatory restoration and stricter scrutiny of new projects in and around these wetlands.

  • Link Red List status to Amrit Dharohar funding and pilots.

    Require that a minimum share of Amrit Dharohar and Amrit Dharohar Capacity Building funds flow to Red List sites each year, beyond the current limited pilot set.

02 Enforce M.K. Balakrishnan and Wetlands Rules, 2017 through data
  • Complete and publish geo‑referenced inventories for all ~2,01,503 mapped wetlands, building on the Supreme Court’s directions.

    Integrate Ramsar and non‑Ramsar wetlands into a single national geodatabase with open access for states, researchers and courts.

  • Use RSIS threat fields as a compliance dashboard.

    Mandate annual updating of threat categories for all Ramsar sites and require state governments to report how each prohibited activity under Rule 4/7 is being prevented or mitigated.

03 Rebalance designation strategy using diversity analytics
  • Set numerical targets for under‑used Ramsar criteria (C1, C7, C8, C9).

    Direct state authorities to identify at least a specified number of new candidate sites for geophysical uniqueness, fish spawning and non‑avian vertebrates by 2030, correcting the current bird‑centric bias.

  • Prioritise missing ecosystem types and regions.

    se your diversity and latitude/area findings to focus new designations on peatlands, desert wetlands, high‑altitude marshes and under‑represented northern riverine systems, rather than only adding more bird sanctuaries.

04 De‑risk anchor basins and urban crisis nodes
  • Prepare site‑specific “Anchor Basin Resilience Plans” for Sundarbans, Chilika, Vembanad‑Kol and other mega‑sites.

    Combine climate scenarios, upstream land‑use regulation, salinity and sediment management, and strict limits on new infrastructure, with measurable indicators tied to GBF “30×30” reporting.

  • Declare Urban Ramsar sites with highest threat density (e.g., Pallikaranai, Thane Creek) as “no‑net‑loss” zones.

    Under the 2017 Rules, prohibit any further permanent conversion and require offsets or restoration that measurably reduce the cumulative threat score over a fixed period (e.g., 5–10 years).

05 Plan at corridor and cluster scales
  • Recognise Ramsar clusters as single ecological complexes in plans and EIAs.

    Tamil Nadu coastal clusters, Nagi–Nakti, and similar pairs should have joint management plans, with connectivity indicators (shared bird counts, groundwater linkages) tracked explicitly.

  • Create buffer and stepping‑stone wetlands around ecological islands.

    Use the distance‑to‑nearest‑neighbour analysis to prioritise new community reserves, OECMs or smaller protected wetlands around sites like Kolleru, Deepor Beel and Loktak, reducing isolation risk.

06 Embed data and transparency in every intervention
  • Standardise a national “Ramsar Metrics Pack”.

    For each site, publish annually: diversity score (criteria), threat count by category, Red‑List status, governance type, and key trends in area/land cover, leveraging remote sensing and community monitoring.

  • Tie finance and tourism permissions to performance.

    Under Amrit Dharohar’s nature‑tourism focus, make continued tourism or new facilities contingent on maintaining or improving diversity scores and reducing threat indices, rather than just visitor numbers.

07 Strengthen local and community governance
  • Mandate co‑management bodies for Red List and anchor sites.

    Implement formal Wetland Management Committees with local communities, fisher groups, and independent scientists, as envisaged under the 2017 Rules and various guidelines.

  • Invest in livelihood transitions that reduce threat density.

    Use Amrit Dharohar’s capacity‑building components (boatman training, alternative livelihoods) explicitly to move people away from the most damaging activities identified in your threat analysis (unregulated construction, intensive agriculture, pollution).

Conclusion: The evaluation concludes that India’s Ramsar policy has moved from slow, emblematic protection to rapid, criteria‑rich expansion, but that the first four decades largely failed to anticipate how urbanisation, infrastructure and climate pressures would converge on the very wetlands of highest ecological value. Going forward, the paper argues for a decisive shift from nominal designation to risk‑aware governance: rebalancing Ramsar criteria toward under‑represented ecosystem functions, de‑risking mega‑anchors, protecting clusters and corridors, and using the Ramsar Red List as a priority list for funding, enforcement, and Amrit Dharohar interventions so that India’s expanded network translates into durable ecological security rather than a larger, more fragile inventory on paper.
