Wetland Risk Assessment: The Red List

We define the "Red List" of Indian Wetlands as those high-value sites that achieve a Diversity Score of 5 or higher while simultaneously facing 20 or more unique threats. These sites represent the most ecologically significant yet vulnerable areas in the country. Based on the data, the following sites are under extreme pressure:

39 THREATS

Pallikaranai Marsh

This is the most stressed wetland in the current assessment. It is experiencing a total system assault, facing everything from urban sprawl and industrial pollution to sensory disturbances (light and noise) and severe climate events.

35 THREATS

Gulf of Mannar

A complex marine ecosystem where heavy industrial activity, international shipping lanes, and climate change pressures converge.

34 THREATS

Nandur Madhameshwar

This site is suffering from severe fragmentation due to a high density of infrastructure, such as roads and utility lines, combined with relentless agricultural encroachment.

29 THREATS

Asan & Beas Reserves

These riverine systems are primarily stressed by hydrological disruptions, which include water regulation, dams, and canalization, compounded by heavy recreational and tourism use.

The analysis reveals that India's Ramsar sites are not merely facing isolated environmental problems, but compound crises. Sites like the Pallikaranai Marsh are navigating nearly 40 distinct threats simultaneously. As a wetland falls within an urban or industrial sphere, the number of threats does not simply increase—it multiplies. This implies that traditional conservation methods, which had narrow focus like protecting the water body itself, are insufficient. To preserve these ecological hubs, management must shift towards integrated mitigation strategies to address the surrounding urban and industrial infrastructure as part of the broader ecosystem.

Actionable Policy Recommendations

01 Legalise the Ramsar Red List Sites as priority wetlands
  • Notify Red List sites as priority wetlands under existing legal architecture like State Wetland Management Rules (2017) and State Action Plans.

    Use metrics like diversity score and threat counts to create a formal Priority Category, with mandatory restoration and stricter scrutiny of new projects in and around these wetlands.

  • Dedicated corpus to Red List sites under Amrit Dharohar funding.

    Set aside a share of Amrit Dharohar and Amrit Dharohar Capacity Building funds towards restoration and conservation of threatened sites.

02 Enforce and Update Wetlands (Conservation and Management) Rules, 2017.
  • Extend protection to smaller sites.

    Expand protection to smaller wetlands and specific types like salt pans, which were omitted from the 2017 rules.

  • Use RSIS threat fields as a compliance dashboard.

    Mandate annual updating of threat categories for all Ramsar sites and require state governments to report how each prohibited activity under Rule 4/7 is being prevented or mitigated.

03 Rethink designation strategy to rebalance Ramsar network
  • Establish targets for under‑used Ramsar criteria (C1, C7, C8, C9).

    Incentivise States to identify sites under the least-used criteria to rebalance India's Ramsar network towards geophysical uniqueness, fish-spawning and non-avian vertebrates too correct the current bird-centric bias.

  • Prioritise Geocentric sites to compensate for the missing ecosystem types.

    Agenda should be to identify new sites using our latitudinal, and area findings to look for geophysical sites like peatlands, grasslnad wetlands, marshes in high altitudes, and sites along riverine networks.

04 Safeguard mega-sites, anchor hubs and urban wetlands
  • Site‑specific management plans

    Prepare localized, site‑specific management plans for mega sites like, Sundarbans by combining climate resilience, land‑use regulation, salinity and sediment management, and strict caps on new infrastructure projects aligning with SDGs.

  • Emphasize Urban Ramsar sites among Red list sites.

    Classify the most threatened urbn Ramsar sites as protected areas, freezing further conversions, and mandating compensatory restoration measures to reduce threats over a defined timeline.

05 Implement and enforce actions at cluster/corridor levels rather than at isolated nodes
  • Notify Ramsar clusters as ecological complexes.

    Recognise Tamil Nadu coastal clusters, Nagi–Nakti, as Ramsar corridors in plans and EIAs with binding joint management plans, and collective monitoring of indicators.

  • Buffers around isolated sites.

    Utilise findings based on distance‑to‑nearest‑neighbour analysis to create buffers around solitary sites to reduce isolation risks.

06 Intervene based on data-driven, onjective, and transparent insights
  • Standardise data sharing formats.

    Mandate annual information updates in standardised formats on metrics like diversity scores, threat counts, Red list status, and governance leveraging technology like remote sensing and Artificial Intelligence.

  • Tie finance and tourism permissions to performance.

    Make continued tourism and fund flows contingent upon improved performance on metrics.

07 Emphasize local and community governance structures
  • Mandate co‑management bodies for Red List sites.

    Integrate Wetland Management Committees with local communities, fisher groups, and independent scientists, as envisaged under the 2017 Rules.

    Engage local communities through programs like Community-Based Resource Management (CBRM) and Wetland Mitras for local stewardship.

  • Focus and invest in livelihood transitions to reverse wetland degradation.

    Use Amrit Dharohar’s capacity‑building components (boatman training, alternative livelihoods) to move people away from ecosystem threatening activities (unregulated construction, intensive agriculture, pollution).

08 Learning from Best Practices
  • Collaborate with industry leaders and pioneers

    Adopt successful models like China's "Sponge Cities" initiative and the Netherlands' "Room for the River" program for integrated water and flood management.

Conclusion

The evaluation concludes that India’s Ramsar policy has moved from slow, emblematic protection to rapid, criteria‑rich expansion, but that the first four decades largely failed to anticipate how urbanisation, infrastructure and climate pressures would converge on the very wetlands of highest ecological value. Going forward, the paper argues for a decisive shift from nominal designation to risk‑aware governance: rebalancing Ramsar criteria toward under‑represented ecosystem functions, de‑risking mega‑anchors, protecting clusters and corridors, and using the Ramsar Red List as a priority list for funding, enforcement, and Amrit Dharohar interventions so that India’s expanded network translates into durable ecological security rather than a larger, more fragile inventory on paper.

NOTE: This framework is designed as a data‑driven tool that can be updated each year as new Ramsar sites are designated, criteria are revised, or additional threats are reported, ensuring that India’s wetland policy remains responsive to a changing ecological and institutional landscape.
